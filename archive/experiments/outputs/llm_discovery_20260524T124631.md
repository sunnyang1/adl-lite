# Peripheral Attention Trap

## Discovery Statement

In anti-money laundering transaction monitoring, alert triage systems exhibit a systematic bias toward graph-peripheral nodes—entities with low degree centrality but high transactional velocity—while neglecting structurally central nodes that orchestrate fund flows. The bias emerges because peripheral nodes generate frequent, individually suspicious alerts that consume disproportionate analyst attention, effectively shielding the orchestrating core from scrutiny. Over successive triage cycles, sink convergence occurs: investigation resources pool around peripheral noise, and the true coordination hub remains unresolved, allowing laundering networks to persist undetected.

## Intuition

The phenomenon mirrors a classic attention-allocation failure in signal-dense environments. Each peripheral node produces alerts at a rate proportional to transactional velocity, not structural importance. Analysts, operating under time constraints, prioritize the queue head—dominated by high-frequency peripheral alerts—over deeper graph analysis. The result is a self-reinforcing loop: peripheral nodes receive investigation, generate partial SARs, and re-enter the queue, while central orchestrators never surface. The trap is emergent because no single rule or threshold causes the failure; the pattern arises from the interaction of alert volume, triage heuristics, and network topology.
