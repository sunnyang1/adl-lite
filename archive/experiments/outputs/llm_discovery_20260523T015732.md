# Peripheral Attention Trap

## Discovery Statement

In anti-money laundering graph monitoring, attention mechanisms and risk scoring models exhibit a systematic blind spot toward peripheral nodes—entities positioned at the outer edges of transaction networks with few direct connections. When illicit fund flows are deliberately routed through these low-salience peripheral nodes before converging at a central sink account, detection systems fail to assign sufficient risk weight to the peripheral hop. The result is a structural evasion pattern where the peripheral node acts as a "trap" that absorbs investigative attention at the wrong layer, allowing the true aggregation point to escape scrutiny until after funds have dispersed.

## Intuition

Standard AML attention models prioritize high-degree nodes and dense subgraphs. A peripheral node—defined as a graph vertex with degree ≤ 3 and betweenness centrality below the 10th percentile—receives minimal feature enrichment. Laundering networks exploit the monotonic relationship between node degree and risk score: by inserting a low-degree intermediary, the path from origin to sink appears fragmented. The sink account, which may itself be high-degree, receives funds that appear to originate from many unrelated low-risk sources. The "trap" is the false sense of safety conferred by the peripheral layer, which fragments the signal that would otherwise trigger a convergence alert.
