# Peripheral Attention Trap

## Discovery Statement

In AML transaction monitoring, suspicious activity frequently migrates to graph peripheral nodes—entities with low degree centrality and minimal historical alert volume—where detection models allocate disproportionately low attention weight. The resulting blind spot creates a systematic evasion corridor: actors deliberately route funds through low-salience intermediaries, causing sink convergence patterns where illicit value pools accumulate at nodes that evade threshold-based and graph-based alerting. The Peripheral Attention Trap describes the emergent feedback loop in which model retraining on historical alerts further reduces sensitivity to peripheral nodes, reinforcing the evasion corridor over successive monitoring cycles.

## Intuition

Standard AML monitoring architectures prioritize high-degree nodes and known typology matches. When an adversary observes that peripheral entities—newly registered shell companies, dormant accounts, low-volume trade intermediaries—rarely trigger alerts, routing strategies shift accordingly. Over time, illicit fund flows converge on peripheral sinks. The attention allocation mechanism in graph neural network (GNN) detectors and rule-based systems exhibits a form of confirmation bias: low historical alert volume at a node translates to low learned importance, which perpetuates low alert volume. The trap is emergent because no single rule or model component explicitly deprioritizes peripheral nodes; the pattern arises from the interaction of training data sparsity, feature engineering choices, and threshold calibration.
