# Peripheral Attention Trap

## Discovery Statement

In AML transaction monitoring, compliance analyst attention becomes disproportionately concentrated on high-centrality graph nodes (major hubs, known typology matches) while peripheral nodes — low-degree entities at the network edge — accumulate unresolved alerts and quietly converge into sink structures. The monitoring system's own attention allocation heuristic reinforces the blind spot: peripheral alerts receive lower risk scores, enter longer review queues, and eventually time out or auto-close, creating a systematic evasion corridor for actors who deliberately fragment activity across many low-visibility endpoints.

## Intuition

The trap operates through a feedback loop between human cognitive bias and algorithmic scoring. Analysts naturally focus on the loudest signals — large-value transactions, dense clusters, known bad actors. The scoring model, trained on historical analyst decisions, learns to deprioritize peripheral nodes because peripheral nodes historically receive less analyst engagement. Over time, the model's deprioritization further reduces analyst engagement, completing the cycle. Meanwhile, a coordinated actor can exploit the periphery by distributing illicit flows across dozens of low-degree accounts, each individually below attention thresholds but collectively forming a sink that drains value out of the monitored network.

The mechanism is structurally similar to the "long tail" problem in recommendation systems: the head captures all the optimization energy while the tail — where novel or adversarial behavior hides — remains underexplored.
