# Peripheral Attention Trap

## Discovery Statement

In anti-money laundering graph monitoring, attention mechanisms and risk-scoring models exhibit a systematic blind spot toward peripheral nodes that serve as convergence sinks for illicit fund flows. The peripheral attention trap describes the emergent pattern where low-degree nodes positioned at the boundary of transaction networks receive disproportionately low analytical weight, yet function as critical aggregation points for layering-stage capital movements. Detection systems trained on centrality-weighted features consistently under-score these peripheral sinks, allowing launderers to exploit the structural bias by routing funds through seemingly insignificant intermediary accounts before final consolidation.

## Intuition

Standard graph neural network architectures for AML assign attention weights proportional to node degree and local clustering coefficients. Peripheral nodes—those with degree below the network median and minimal participation in dense subgraphs—receive near-zero attention scores during message-passing iterations. However, adversarial actors deliberately construct transaction topologies where peripheral nodes act as "funnel mouths," collecting small-value transfers from many high-degree source nodes before executing a single large outbound transfer. The funnel-mouth topology inverts the expected relationship between structural importance and transactional significance, creating a trap where the monitoring system's own attention allocation becomes the vulnerability.
