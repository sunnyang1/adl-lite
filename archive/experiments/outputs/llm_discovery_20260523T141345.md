# Peripheral Attention Trap

## Discovery Statement

In AML transaction monitoring, compliance teams allocate disproportionate investigative attention to high-centrality nodes (known high-risk entities, major hubs) while systematically under-monitoring peripheral graph nodes that exhibit low individual connectivity but collectively form sink convergence patterns. The peripheral attention trap emerges when structurally peripheral accounts—each appearing benign in isolation—aggregate illicit fund flows through coordinated low-volume, high-frequency transfers that route capital toward a small set of sink nodes. Because each individual peripheral edge falls below alert thresholds and the peripheral nodes lack direct links to flagged entities, the entire convergence structure evades standard rule-based and centrality-weighted monitoring systems. The trap is self-reinforcing: as investigators focus attention on central nodes, peripheral convergence patterns deepen undetected, eventually enabling large-scale capital outflows through what appear to be unrelated low-risk channels.

## Intuition

Standard AML graph analysis prioritizes degree centrality, betweenness, and direct adjacency to known suspicious nodes. The peripheral attention trap exploits a blind spot in such prioritization: the assumption that low-centrality nodes pose low aggregate risk. When dozens or hundreds of peripheral accounts each receive small inbound transfers from diverse sources and forward funds to a common sink node, the per-transaction risk score remains low while the collective pattern constitutes a classic layering structure. The monitoring system's attention allocation mechanism—whether human analyst triage or automated scoring—treats each peripheral node independently, missing the emergent convergence topology. The trap is analogous to a distributed denial-of-service attack in network security: no single source appears malicious, but the coordinated effect overwhelms the target's defenses.
