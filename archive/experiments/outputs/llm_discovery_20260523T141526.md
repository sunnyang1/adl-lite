# Peripheral Attention Trap

## Discovery Statement

In AML transaction monitoring, the Peripheral Attention Trap describes a structural vulnerability where illicit fund flows deliberately route through low-salience graph nodes—entities or accounts positioned at the periphery of the attention-weighted network. Standard rule-based and even attention-based detection models allocate disproportionate analytical weight to high-degree hub nodes and known typology anchors, leaving peripheral nodes under-sampled and under-reviewed. Adversarial actors exploit the gradient by staging layering sequences across peripheral nodes, allowing illicit capital to reach convergence sinks (final aggregation accounts) with minimal detection friction. The trap emerges not from model failure but from the inherent geometry of attention allocation in sparse, heterogeneous financial graphs.

## Intuition

The core mechanism relies on an isomorphism between attention-weighted graph neural networks and human analyst triage. Both systems exhibit a "center-periphery" bias: nodes with high betweenness centrality or direct links to flagged entities receive disproportionate scrutiny. Adversarial actors construct transaction chains that minimize centrality scores at each hop, effectively "hiding in the noise floor" of the monitoring system. The convergence sink—where fragmented peripheral flows reassemble—remains invisible until the final aggregation event, by which point the layering is complete. The trap is emergent because no single peripheral transaction triggers an alert; the pattern only becomes legible when the full peripheral-to-sink topology is reconstructed retrospectively.
