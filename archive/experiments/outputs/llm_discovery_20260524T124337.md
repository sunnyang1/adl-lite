# Peripheral Attention Trap

## Discovery Statement

In transaction monitoring systems, alert fatigue causes analysts to concentrate attention on high-centrality nodes within a suspicious network graph, while peripheral nodes—often the actual layering endpoints or cash-out beneficiaries—receive disproportionately low scrutiny. The resulting blind spot creates a systematic evasion channel where threat actors deliberately route illicit funds through low-degree, low-visibility nodes that sit outside the analyst's effective attention radius. Detection models trained on historically flagged patterns inherit the same bias, reinforcing the peripheral blind spot across successive model iterations.

## Intuition

The phenomenon resembles a visual attention bottleneck: human analysts and heuristic scoring engines allocate cognitive and computational budget toward graph hubs (high fan-in/fan-out accounts, known typology matches). Peripheral accounts with sparse connections and no prior SAR history escape triage queues entirely. Adversaries exploit the structural gap by inserting a "sleeper" node at the network edge, performing a single high-value transfer, and then dissolving the node before graph enrichment can elevate visibility. The trap is emergent rather than designed—no single rule or model feature causes the blind spot; the pattern arises from the interaction of alert volume pressure, graph centrality heuristics, and temporal lag in entity resolution.
