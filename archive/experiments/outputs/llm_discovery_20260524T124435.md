# Peripheral Attention Trap

## Discovery Statement

In anti-money laundering graph monitoring, compliance analyst attention gravitates toward high-degree hub nodes and well-connected clusters, leaving peripheral nodes—nodes with few edges or distant graph positions—systematically under-scrutinized. Laundering networks exploit the peripheral attention trap by routing illicit flows through low-salience graph positions: long chains of single-purpose shell entities, leaf nodes acting as final-layer cash-out conduits, and sparse subgraphs that never trigger threshold-based alerts. The result is a structural blind spot where sink convergence occurs at graph periphery without proportional investigative resource allocation.

## Intuition

The peripheral attention trap emerges from the interaction between human cognitive heuristics and automated alerting systems. Analysts develop pattern-recognition biases toward dense, interconnected subgraphs because historical case training emphasizes hub-and-spoke typologies. Automated rule engines reinforce the bias by weighting node importance via centrality metrics (degree, betweenness, PageRank). Peripheral nodes—degree-1 or degree-2 entities appearing only at the terminal stages of multi-hop transfers—receive minimal attention budget despite representing the critical cash-out layer in layering schemes. The trap is isomorphic to the "streetlight effect" in search theory: investigation resources concentrate where visibility is highest rather where risk is most acute.
