# Peripheral Attention Trap

## Discovery Statement

In AML transaction monitoring, a recurring failure mode emerges when compliance systems allocate disproportionate investigative resources to graph-peripheral nodes—entities with high transaction volume but low structural centrality—while critical laundering pathways consolidate through sink convergence points that receive minimal analytical attention. The pattern creates a systematic blind spot where illicit funds exit the network through nodes that appear benign under standard risk scoring, because peripheral status paradoxically reduces suspicion despite high throughput.

## Intuition

Standard AML monitoring frameworks weight attention based on transaction velocity and explicit risk indicators. Graph-peripheral nodes—entities positioned at the edges of transaction networks with few direct connections to flagged accounts—often accumulate significant volume without triggering alerts. Meanwhile, sink convergence points where multiple peripheral flows merge into final destination accounts receive aggregated attention only after consolidation, by which point the laundering cycle nears completion. The "trap" lies in the inverse relationship between structural visibility and actual risk: peripheral nodes attract scrutiny through volume metrics, while convergence sinks evade detection through apparent normalcy until late-stage pattern recognition activates.
