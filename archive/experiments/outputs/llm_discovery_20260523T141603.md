# Peripheral Attention Trap

## Discovery Statement

In AML transaction monitoring, alert triage systems exhibit a systematic bias toward graph-central nodes (high-degree hubs, frequent counterparties) while neglecting structurally peripheral nodes that serve as convergence sinks for laundered funds. The monitoring attention allocation follows a power-law distribution that mirrors the transaction graph degree distribution, creating blind spots at the network periphery where layering operations terminate. Laundering networks exploit the peripheral attention trap by routing final-stage consolidation through low-degree entities that receive minimal analyst scrutiny.

## Intuition

The peripheral attention trap emerges from the interaction between graph topology and human cognitive load. When analysts face high alert volumes, attention naturally gravitates toward visually prominent nodes in network visualizations—nodes with many connections. Peripheral nodes, despite being structurally critical as fund sinks, appear insignificant in standard graph layouts. The trap is isomorphic to the "edge effect" in ecological monitoring, where biodiversity hotspots at habitat boundaries receive disproportionate neglect.
