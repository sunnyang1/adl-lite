# Peripheral Attention Trap

## Discovery Statement

In AML transaction monitoring, alert triage systems exhibit a systematic bias toward high-centrality nodes in the transaction graph, causing peripheral nodes—entities with low degree but high-value sink convergence—to escape scrutiny. The peripheral attention trap describes the emergent failure mode where compliance analysts and automated scoring models allocate disproportionate cognitive and computational resources to well-connected hub accounts while structurally similar laundering pathways route funds through low-attention peripheral entities that aggregate into final sink accounts. Detection rates for peripheral-path laundering drop significantly compared to hub-based schemes, creating a predictable blind spot exploitable by sophisticated actors.

## Intuition

The peripheral attention trap arises from the interaction between graph-based risk scoring and human attention allocation. Standard centrality measures (PageRank, betweenness) elevate hub nodes in alert queues, while peripheral nodes receive minimal triage priority. Laundering networks exploit the structural gap: funds traverse multiple low-degree intermediaries before converging at sink accounts. The convergence pattern—many peripheral inputs feeding a small set of sinks—remains invisible when each peripheral edge is evaluated independently rather than as a collective convergence signal. The trap deepens when feedback loops reinforce the bias: low-priority alerts for peripheral nodes generate fewer SARs, which in turn reduce training signal for models, perpetuating under-detection.
