# Peripheral Attention Trap

## Discovery Statement

In AML transaction monitoring, graph peripheral nodes—entities with low direct alert volume but high structural connectivity to flagged clusters—absorb disproportionate investigative attention through a sink convergence effect. Compliance analysts repeatedly route peripheral entities into enhanced due diligence queues not because of direct suspicious indicators, but because graph-based scoring algorithms propagate suspicion scores outward from core flagged nodes. The resulting attention allocation creates a systematic blind spot: resources consumed by peripheral review reduce capacity for detecting novel typologies emerging in unscored graph regions.

## Intuition

The trap operates through an isomorphism with the "streetlight effect" in observational science. AML monitoring systems illuminate graph neighborhoods around known suspicious patterns with high resolution, while leaving distant graph regions in darkness. Analysts, constrained by queue capacity and SLA pressure, naturally gravitate toward the well-lit periphery rather than venturing into unmonitored territory. Over successive review cycles, peripheral node review becomes a ritualized activity—high volume, low conversion, self-reinforcing through feedback loops that confirm the algorithm's suspicion propagation model.

The mechanism mirrors attention sink phenomena observed in transformer architectures, where initial tokens absorb disproportionate attention weights regardless of semantic relevance. In the AML graph context, nodes adjacent to flagged clusters become "attention sinks" for investigative resources.
