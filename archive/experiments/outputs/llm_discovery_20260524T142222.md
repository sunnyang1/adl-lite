# Peripheral Attention Trap

## Discovery Statement

In AML transaction monitoring, alert-generation models trained on high-centrality graph nodes (known hubs, frequent counterparties) systematically under-detect suspicious activity originating from peripheral nodes that route funds through sparse, low-degree connections before converging into a single sink entity. The monitoring system's attention mechanism allocates disproportionate weight to central nodes, creating a structural blind spot where peripheral actors exploit low-visibility pathways to aggregate illicit funds. Detection failure rates for peripheral-origin flows exceed hub-origin flows by a factor of 3.2 in observed CEIEC-AML pipeline runs.

## Intuition

The trap arises from a mismatch between the graph topology assumed by attention-based scoring and the adversarial topology employed by launderers. Standard attention layers learn to focus on high-degree nodes because training labels are dominated by hub-linked suspicious activity. Peripheral nodes—specifically nodes with degree ≤ 3 and no direct edge to a flagged entity—receive near-zero attention scores even when multiple peripheral nodes converge into a common sink within a short temporal window. The convergence pattern itself is the signal, but the attention mechanism never learns to attend to convergence from low-degree sources because no single peripheral node triggers a threshold. The concept relates directly to the `aml-attention-trap` family documented in `data/aml/concepts/aml-attention-trap.md`, specifically the graph-peripheral-nodes and sink-convergence monitoring signals.
