# Peripheral Attention Trap

## Discovery Statement

In AML transaction monitoring, alert-generation models and human analysts allocate disproportionate investigative resources to entities occupying peripheral positions in a transaction graph—nodes with low degree centrality but high betweenness centrality that serve as bridges between otherwise disconnected clusters. The peripheral attention trap describes the emergent failure mode where such bridge nodes absorb investigative attention because graph-based risk scores surface the bridge nodes as structurally suspicious, even when the underlying transactional behavior is benign. Meanwhile, the true high-risk entities operating within dense, well-connected clusters evade scrutiny because cluster-internal activity appears self-consistent and does not trigger peripheral-node heuristics. The trap is self-reinforcing: each investigation of a peripheral node generates training labels that further bias future models toward peripheral-node alerting, creating a feedback loop that systematically deprioritizes core-cluster risk.

## Intuition

The peripheral attention trap arises from a confluence of graph-theoretic bias and human cognitive anchoring. Graph-based AML models commonly compute risk using metrics such as PageRank, betweenness centrality, and community-bridge scores. Nodes sitting at the boundary between communities—often correspondents, small remittance houses, or newly onboarded counterparties—receive elevated scores because the boundary nodes mediate inter-community flow. Analysts, presented with a ranked queue, naturally begin investigation at the top of the list, where peripheral nodes cluster. Each closed case involving a peripheral node reinforces the model's belief that peripheral positions correlate with risk. Over successive retraining cycles, the model's decision boundary shifts to capture more peripheral nodes and fewer core-cluster nodes, even when the core clusters contain the actual layering or structuring activity. The result is a monitoring regime structurally blind to the dense, internally consistent typologies favored by sophisticated laundering networks.
