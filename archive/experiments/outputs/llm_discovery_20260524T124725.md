# Peripheral Attention Trap

## Discovery Statement

In transaction monitoring systems, illicit actors exploit the periphery of the attention graph — low-priority nodes that receive minimal scrutiny — to stage fund movements before converging into a single high-risk sink node. The pattern emerges when peripheral nodes individually fall below alert thresholds, yet collectively form a coordinated funnel. Detection systems trained on central-node heuristics systematically miss the peripheral staging phase, creating a structural blind spot analogous to the "attention trap" in adversarial machine learning.

## Intuition

Standard AML monitoring allocates computational and human attention to nodes with high centrality: large-value accounts, frequent transactors, and known-risk entities. Peripheral nodes — dormant accounts, low-volume wallets, newly opened shells — sit outside the focal cone. Adversaries exploit the gradient of attention by distributing small, sub-threshold transactions across many peripheral nodes. Each individual edge appears benign. The convergence event, when peripheral funds aggregate into a sink, triggers a single alert — but by then the laundering topology is already established and the provenance chain is fragmented across dozens of low-signal paths. The "trap" is the false confidence that peripheral silence equals safety.
