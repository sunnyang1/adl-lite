# Peripheral Attention Trap

## Discovery Statement

In AML transaction monitoring, alert triage systems exhibit a systematic bias toward graph-peripheral nodes—entities with high betweenness centrality but low direct risk scores—when sink convergence patterns emerge across multiple laundering pathways. The monitoring pipeline allocates disproportionate analyst attention to graph-peripheral nodes, creating a blind spot where core orchestrators embedded in dense subgraphs evade scrutiny. The trap activates when three conditions co-occur: (1) peripheral node degree exceeds a local threshold, (2) at least two independent sink clusters converge on the same ultimate beneficiary, and (3) the attention budget allocated to peripheral investigation exceeds 40% of total triage capacity. Under such conditions, core nodes experience a measurable drop in detection latency, allowing laundering operations to complete settlement before flag propagation reaches the orchestrator layer.

## Intuition

The mechanism resembles a classic "shiny object" effect in network security monitoring. Graph-peripheral nodes generate high volumes of low-confidence alerts because peripheral positioning maximizes exposure to diverse transaction types. Each alert carries a small attention cost, and the cumulative cost across many peripheral nodes consumes the analyst bandwidth needed for deep subgraph analysis. The convergence of sink pathways amplifies the effect: when two or more peripheral chains terminate at the same beneficiary, the system interprets convergence as signal strength rather than as evidence of deliberate structuring. Analysts then spend cycles tracing peripheral chains backward, while the orchestrator—positioned in a dense, low-alert subgraph—remains invisible until settlement completes.
