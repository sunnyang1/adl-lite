ADL Lite presents a Markdown-native document contract for multi-agent concept consensus and evaluates four pilot questions, with RQ1 showing that LLM-as-judge referent clarity on `n=15` discoveries is identical between ADL and fair plain (`mean_delta_adl_minus_plain=0.0` for both judges, `mean_across_judges_adl=4.3333`) while the unstructured plain-LLM comparator scores lower (`mean_across_judges_plain_llm=3.0`, ADL-minus-plain-LLM mean deltas `1.0667` and `1.6`).  
For RQ2, results indicate that ADL provides explicit lifecycle traceability by recording `8` scripted consensus transitions with `3` validated documents versus `0` in plain Markdown, while a separate MiMo batch reports `n=10`, mean transitions `2.0`, success rate `1.0`, mean attempts `1.7`, and revised rate `0.7`, emphasizing measurable auditability even when workloads differ.  
For RQ3, ablation metrics show full-set retrieval gains at `k=10` (`n_queries=25`, hit recall `1.0` vs `0.8`, delta `+0.2`; label recall `0.9667` vs `0.7267`, delta `+0.24`) but also localize the strongest effect to the L3-only subset (`n=5`, hit and label deltas `+1.0`) while scenario-only queries (`n=20`) show `scenario_hit_delta=0.0` and `scenario_label_delta=0.05`.  
For RQ4, scope controls deny `60/60` cross-scope probes with `adl_leaks=0`, and the overall evidence should be interpreted as pilot-limited because key claims depend on bounded sample sizes (`n=15` judge pass), an L3-only five-query ablation slice, and proxy LLM adjudication plus fixture-backed plain-LLM labels rather than large-scale human-rated evaluations.
