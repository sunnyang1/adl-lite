\subsection{Agent Governance Landscape}\label{subsec:agent-governance}

The proliferation of LLM agents across enterprise and open-source ecosystems has created an urgent need for governance infrastructure that goes beyond traditional ontology engineering. Recent work has identified three complementary layers of agent governance: trust and permissioning, architecture-level safety, and provenance tracking. ADL Lite addresses a gap that none of these layers fills: a lightweight, event-sourced, verifiable registry of agent capabilities and their lifecycle states.

\textbf{KYA} \cite{kya2025} provides a framework-agnostic trust layer for LLM agents. It uses HMAC chains to verify agent identity and action authenticity, achieving approximately 1,800 operations per second across more than 15 framework adapters (available as \texttt{veldt-kya} on PyPI). KYA's scope is permissions and trust establishment: it records \emph{who} performed an action and whether the action is authorized. However, KYA does not govern the lifecycle of the capabilities themselves---there is no mechanism for tracking how a tool's advertised functionality evolves from registration through validation, deprecation, or fork.

\textbf{AgentSafe} \cite{agentsafe2025} proposes a unified governance framework spanning design-time, runtime, and audit phases. It addresses architecture-level concerns such as model card validation, prompt injection detection, and multi-agent coordination policies. AgentSafe is comprehensive but heavyweight: it requires dedicated infrastructure and does not support Markdown-native authoring or lightweight deployment. ADL Lite is complementary rather than competing: AgentSafe governs the architecture; ADL Lite governs the artifacts that architecture produces.

\textbf{From Agent Traces to Trust} \cite{agenttraces2025} surveys the landscape of agent provenance and explicitly concludes that ``a unified trace schema is still needed.'' ADL Lite is precisely that schema: an event-first, cryptographically linked trace format in which every capability claim, validation, deprecation, and fork is a structured event with deterministic derivation semantics.

\textbf{Talukdar et al.} \cite{talukdar2025} demonstrate multi-agent LLM ontology engineering using a four-role pipeline (Domain Expert $\rightarrow$ Manager $\rightarrow$ Coder $\rightarrow$ QA). This approach produces OWL ontologies at high velocity but provides no lifecycle governance for the resulting artifacts: once produced, the ontology is static. ADL Lite wraps such outputs with an append-only, auditable event chain that records every subsequent validation, challenge, and revision.

\textbf{SafeAgent} \cite{safeagent2025} defines a safety protocol for LLM agents, focusing on protocol-level constraints such as output filtering and action sandboxing. Like KYA, SafeAgent operates at the interaction layer rather than the artifact layer: it governs what an agent \emph{may} do, not what a capability \emph{is} or how it \emph{evolves}.

\textbf{Positioning statement.} ADL Lite is not a fourth route in LLM-native ontology engineering. It is a complementary governance layer that adds event-sourced, lifecycle-aware, verifiable capability tracking to whatever ontologies agents produce through any route. Where KYA answers ``Is this agent trusted?'' and AgentSafe answers ``Is this architecture safe?'' and Talukdar et al.\ answer ``How do we build ontologies with agents?'' ADL Lite answers ``How do we record, validate, and govern the capabilities that agents advertise?''

\subsection{Foundational Ontologies and Event-First Semantics}\label{subsec:foundational-ontologies}

Foundational ontologies provide top-level categories that constrain domain ontology development. BFO distinguishes continuants from occurrents; ADL Lite's \texttt{Event} corresponds to BFO's \emph{process} and its capability lifecycle events to BFO's \emph{occurrent} category---processes that unfold in time and depend on participants \cite{bfo}. DOLCE's \emph{perdurant} corresponds to BFO's occurrent and ADL Lite's \texttt{Event}, while its \emph{non-physical object} captures ADL Lite capabilities as information objects carrying social weight \cite{dolce}. UFO's \emph{relator} category aligns with ADL Lite's L3 relation blocks, and its event/action distinction maps to ADL Lite's lifecycle vs.~communication event partition \cite{ufo}. Table~\ref{tab:foundational-comparison} summarizes the cross-mapping; detailed analysis and justification of alignment choices appear in Section~\ref{sec:ontological-analysis}.

ADL Lite does not fully align with any single foundational ontology; it adopts a \emph{pragmatic pluralism}: BFO's continuant/occurrent distinction as conceptual guide, DOLCE's descriptive adequacy as design principle, and UFO's event/action distinction as modeling framework. This pluralism is deliberate: the target domain requires a lightweight ontology authored in Markdown, not a heavyweight foundational ontology requiring expert curation in OWL. The cost is that ADL Lite cannot directly import BFO, DOLCE, or UFO modules without translation; the benefit is optimization for its intended use case.

\subsection{Knowledge Graph Construction and Ontology Engineering}\label{subsec:kg-ontology-engineering}

The Semantic Web vision \cite{bernerslee2001semanticweb} was operationalized through OWL~2, whose description-logic semantics provide expressiveness from OWL~2~EL to OWL~2~DL \cite{owl2}. Standard toolchains (Prot\'{e}g\'{e}, Apache Jena, Virtuoso) enable ontology authoring and SPARQL query evaluation at scale, but reasoners such as HermiT and Pellet impose computational costs that grow poorly with rapidly evolving knowledge bases \cite{glimm2014hermit}. Triple stores require dedicated infrastructure; OWL reasoners impose batch-oriented computation ill-suited to streaming workloads; and the expertise required creates a bottleneck between ontology engineering and application development \cite{hogan2021knowledge}.

LLM agents produce conceptual artifacts at high velocity; traditional ontology engineering workflows cannot govern their lifecycle. An agent may generate a new capability description, validate it against a domain ontology, and publish it to a shared registry within seconds---a pace that batch-oriented OWL reasoning pipelines and manual curation workflows cannot match. The gap is not in producing ontologies but in governing them: recording what was produced, by whom, with what confidence, and how it evolves over time.

Lightweight alternatives trade expressiveness for accessibility. Schema.org provides a pragmatic vocabulary adopted by over 30\% of web pages \cite{guha2016schemaorg}. JSON-LD extends JSON with namespace support \cite{json_ld}. SHACL provides data validation without inference \cite{w3c_shacl}. These lower the barrier to structured data publishing but remain bound to the RDF data model, and thus exclude domain knowledge encoded in Markdown. The OBO Foundry provides versioning and identifier permanence through centralized release pipelines \cite{obo_foundry}; ADL Lite shares this commitment but uses decentralized append-only EventChains with cryptographic hash linkage. Markdown-based tools (Foam, Obsidian, Dendron) \cite{foam2020,dendron2020} demonstrate that semantic structure can emerge from authoring conventions, but they lack machine-checkable coordination primitives: no constraint language prevents schema drift, and no consensus mechanism mediates conflicting contributions. ADL Lite builds directly on this document-native paradigm while addressing each of these gaps.

\subsubsection{LLM-Native Ontology Engineering}\label{subsubsec:llm-native-oe}

Recent work has explored LLM-native approaches to ontology engineering. LLM4ACOE \cite{llm4acoe} uses LLMs to simulate three roles---Knowledge Engineer, Domain Expert, and Knowledge Worker---generating domain ontologies automatically, but produces standard OWL without cryptographic provenance or lifecycle governance. Sim-HCOME \cite{sim_hcome} extends the HCONE methodology with automated LLM role-playing, but similarly produces static OWL files without append-only audit trails. Ontology-Constrained Neural Reasoning \cite{tuan2026ontology} uses ontological constraints to limit LLM agent behavior, but requires a full OWL reasoning stack. These approaches establish three research routes: (1)~fully automated multi-agent collaboration producing OWL; (2)~LLM-assisted traditional OE workflows; and (3)~ontology-constrained agent behavior. ADL Lite is a governance layer that can wrap and govern ontologies produced by any of these three routes, not a competing production method. It adds event-sourced provenance, cryptographic integrity, and deterministic lifecycle derivation to whatever artifacts agents produce.


\subsection{Provenance, Trust, and Verifiable Publishing}\label{subsec:provenance-trust}

The Semantic Web provenance landscape addresses complementary aspects of ADL Lite's target problem: recording \emph{who} said \emph{what}, \emph{when}, and with \emph{what} evidentiary basis. W3C PROV-O~\cite{w3c_prov_o} provides a standard model for provenance interchange but lacks built-in cryptographic integrity. Linked Data Proofs~\cite{linked_data_proofs} bind actor identities to events through digital signatures over canonicalized RDF---a capability planned for ADL Lite Phase~3. RDF-star~\cite{rdf_star} enables statement-level annotation, analogous to ADL Lite's L3 relation blocks. Blockchain-based systems provide tamper-evident audit trails but impose deployment costs that are unnecessary for small-team governance. Git's signed-commit workflow provides a close operational analog: both rely on append-only history with cryptographic integrity, but ADL Lite adds deterministic status derivation, confidence aggregation, and structured preconditions. \textbf{Git-Native Knowledge Systems.} Beyond Markdown-based note-taking tools, several systems leverage Git for knowledge management. RO-Crate \cite{ro_crate} packages research data with structured metadata in JSON-LD, using Git for versioning but lacking native lifecycle governance or precondition enforcement. DataLad \cite{datalad} extends Git with dataset versioning and provenance tracking, targeting neuroimaging workflows rather than ontology governance. TerminusDB \cite{terminusdb} stores RDF data in a Git-like immutable graph database with branch/merge semantics, but requires a dedicated database server and JSON-LD authoring, conflicting with Markdown-native workflows. These systems share ADL Lite's commitment to immutable history and collaborative versioning, but none combines document-native authoring with deterministic lifecycle derivation and cryptographically linked event chains.

\textbf{Institutional Semantics and Normative Constraints.} Searle's theory of institutional facts \cite{searle1995construction} provides philosophical grounding for ADL Lite's precondition system: a concept's status (e.g., \texttt{validated}) is an institutional fact that exists because the community collectively accepts the constitutive rules governing status transitions. ADL Lite's precondition rules are precisely such constitutive rules: they define that a \texttt{VALIDATE} event counts as a status change to \texttt{validated} only when the preconditions (\texttt{status == provisional}, confidence $\geq 0.5$) are satisfied. This alignment with institutional semantics contextualizes the ``governed lifecycle'' perspective: the EventChain is not merely a log of events but a record of institutional facts constituted by normative rules. Future work (FW6) will explore tighter integration with deontic logic frameworks for expressing obligation, permission, and prohibition over event sequences.


\subsection{Event-Centric Ontologies}\label{subsec:event-centric}

Existing approaches vary in their treatment of events. This subsection examines event-centric approaches that share ADL Lite's premise that \emph{change in the world is best understood through events} rather than through direct property mutations of persistent objects.

\subsubsection{CIDOC CRM, SEM/LODE, and RDF Stream Processing}\label{subsubsec:cidoc-crm}

The CIDOC Conceptual Reference Model \cite{cidoc_crm} is fundamentally event-centric: it models reality through the \texttt{E5 Event} class that brings together actors and objects in spatiotemporal contexts. ADL Lite shares this philosophical stance---lifecycle changes are explicit events rather than mutable field updates---but differs in purpose: CIDOC CRM is \emph{descriptive} (documenting what happened), while ADL Lite is \emph{operational} (enforcing what can happen through preconditions). The Simple Event Model (SEM) \cite{vanhage2011sem} and LODE \cite{shaw2011lode} provide lightweight vocabularies for publishing event data in RDF, but offer no constraint mechanisms for governing event sequences or providing integrity guarantees. RDF Stream Processing \cite{w3c_rsp} enables continuous query evaluation over timestamped RDF triples (C-SPARQL \cite{c_sparql}, CQELS \cite{lephuoc2011cqels}), but targets real-time pattern detection rather than retrospective provenance verification. ADL Lite complements these frameworks with an \emph{operational} layer: the EventChain is a tamper-evident, precondition-governed mechanism for controlling concept lifecycle transitions.

\textbf{Event sourcing as software engineering antecedent.} ADL Lite's EventChain is structurally identical to the \emph{event-sourced aggregate root} pattern in software engineering \cite{event_sourcing}: an append-only sequence of domain events from which application state is derived by replay. Mature event-store implementations (EventStoreDB, Axon Framework, Apache Kafka log compaction) provide high-throughput append-only logs with optimistic concurrency control, but lack the ontological analysis, cryptographic hash chaining, and deterministic lifecycle derivation that ADL Lite provides. We explicitly distinguish ADL Lite's contributions from those of generic event sourcing: (i)~an ontological analysis that situates the event-sourcing pattern within BFO, DOLCE, and UFO; (ii)~cryptographic SHA-256 hash chaining for tamper evidence; (iii)~deterministic lifecycle derivation functions ($\delta$, $\gamma$) with proven properties; and (iv)~Markdown-native authoring that lowers the barrier to collaborative ontology governance.

\subsubsection{Subject-Event Ontology, Occurrence-Only Modeling, and UFO-B}\label{subsubsec:seo-occurrence}

Three recent proposals share ADL Lite's commitment to event-first modeling but pursue different formal foundations. Boldachev's Reactive Subject-Event Ontology (SEO) \cite{boldachev2025seo} uses a causal DAG (events as nodes, happens-before relation as edges) with reactive guards; it excels in distributed concurrent scenarios where multiple agents contribute events without global coordination. ADL Lite's linear hash chain prioritizes sequential audit and cryptographic tamper evidence over partial-order expressiveness---a deliberate trade-off for concept lifecycle governance, where total order carries semantic weight (a concept must be \texttt{REGISTER}ed before \texttt{VALIDATE}d). Al-Fedaghi's ``occurrence-only'' paradigm \cite{alfedaghi2024occurrence} provides independent philosophical grounding for the event-first stance using ``region'' (static blueprint) and ``breath'' (dynamic temporal span) categories. Guizzardi et al.'s UFO-B executable specifications \cite{guizzardi2024ufob} extend UFO-B with ``Social Relator,'' ``Disposition,'' and ``Institutional Event'' as executable event-log specifications; ADL Lite's L4 Action blocks correspond to UFO-B ``Institutional Events,'' but ADL Lite provides lightweight YAML-declared preconditions rather than a full first-order logic axiomatization.

\subsubsection{Conflict-Free Replicated Data Types}\label{subsubsec:crdts}

CRDTs \cite{crdts} provide a theoretical foundation for reconciling concurrent updates without centralized coordination. Automerge \cite{automerge} and Yjs \cite{yjs} are widely deployed CRDT frameworks for real-time collaborative editing. Recent work has explored CRDTs for knowledge graph editing \cite{crdt_rdf_2023} and formally verified CRDT convergence in Iris separation logic \cite{nieto2024crdt}. Recent work has also explored real-time collaborative ontology editing using distributed version control techniques \cite{hemid2024ontoeditor}, which uses Operational Transformation (OT) for concurrent editing. ADL Lite complements such systems with its append-only event chain and deterministic lifecycle governance. ADL Lite's Git-native design provides branch-based concurrent authoring; CRDTs would contribute automatic merge semantics for event chains, eliminating the last-write-wins limitation without sacrificing tamper evidence. Blocklace \cite{blocklace2024} proposes a cryptographic hash DAG as a universal CRDT capable of detecting Byzantine nodes---a direction scoped to Phase~3.

\subsection{Positioning}\label{subsec:positioning}

Palantir Foundry represents the most prominent industrial embodiment of an operational ontology platform. Its Data Engine exposes four core ontological primitives: Object Type, Property, Link, and Action \cite{palantir_fde}. This architecture unifies ``semantic elements'' (nouns) with ``kinetic elements'' (verbs), enabling AI agents to both query and act upon the ontology through governed interfaces \cite{palantir_fde}. Despite its sophistication, Palantir exhibits three limitations: enterprise-scale deployment cost, proprietary ontology authoring tooling, and no design for LLM-native authorship. ADL Lite addresses each by offering pip-installable deployment, Markdown-native authoring, and explicit multi-agent concept consensus.

We argue that ADL Lite occupies a deliberate middle ground. Palantir delivers industrial-strength operational semantics, yet its deployment cost and accessibility barriers exclude small teams and LLM-native workflows. OWL/SPARQL pipelines offer unmatched formal expressiveness yet require specialized expertise and infrastructure. KYA provides lightweight trust verification but does not govern capability lifecycles. AgentSafe delivers comprehensive architecture governance but requires heavyweight infrastructure. Markdown-based tools lower the barrier to knowledge capture but provide no machine-checkable guarantees against schema drift or conflicting definitions.

The gap that ADL Lite fills is the intersection of four properties that, to our knowledge, no existing approach combines: (a)~document-native authoring in plain Markdown, (b)~built-in tamper detection through cryptographic hash chaining, (c)~lifecycle state machines with declarative preconditions, and (d)~pip-installable deployment requiring no enterprise infrastructure. PROV-O provides (a) only when combined with RDF authoring tools and lacks (b) natively. Nanopublications provide (b) for published claims but lack (a), (c), and (d). CIDOC CRM provides event-centric descriptive modeling (ISO 21127), yet its deployment with full RDF/OWL toolchains---including triple stores and SPARQL endpoints---may exceed the infrastructure budget of small teams. ADL Lite enables multiple LLM agents proposing, challenging, and refining concepts within a shared document-native ontology, with the EventChain providing the audit trail and consensus mechanism that makes decentralized authorship accountable.
