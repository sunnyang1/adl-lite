\subsection{Capability-First Governance: Ontological Implications}\label{subsec:ontological-implications}

The empirical results provide support for the event-first design, but the deeper contribution lies in the ontological analysis. By treating events as the fundamental primitive, ADL Lite inverts the traditional object-first hierarchy and raises three ontological questions that merit discussion.

\textbf{Question 1: What is the ontological status of a capability?} In an object-first ontology, a capability is a continuant: it exists in full at every moment, and its properties change over time. In ADL Lite, a capability is a generically dependent continuant that depends on its EventChain for existence. This raises the question: does the capability exist before any events have occurred? The answer is no: a capability comes into existence at the moment of its genesis event (\texttt{REGISTER}). Prior to that event, there is no capability---only the potential for one. This is analogous to how, in BFO, a process comes into existence at the moment it begins: there is no ``pre-existing'' process waiting to unfold.

\textbf{Question 2: Are status and confidence real properties or derived properties of a capability?} In an object-first ontology, status is a real property that inheres in the capability. In ADL Lite, status is a derived property: it is computed by $\delta(C)$ and has no existence independent of the events that constitute the chain. This aligns with the DOLCE view that some properties are ``constructed'' by cognitive agents rather than discovered in the world \cite{dolce}. A capability's status is not a feature of reality; it is a feature of the community's judgment about the capability, as recorded in the event history.

\textbf{Question 3: What happens to a capability when it is deprecated?} In an object-first ontology, deprecation might be modeled as a change of a status field. In ADL Lite, deprecation is the appendage of a \texttt{DEPRECATE} event to the chain. The capability continues to exist (it retains its \texttt{concept\_id}), but its status changes to \texttt{deprecated}. The historical events remain intact, providing a complete audit trail of why the capability was deprecated and by whom. This preserves the ``right to be forgotten'' in reverse: the capability is not erased, but it is explicitly marked as no longer endorsed.

\subsection{Addressing Reviewer Questions}\label{subsec:reviewer-questions}

The following eight questions were raised by the reviewers during the peer-review process. Each is addressed here in summary form, with cross-references to the sections and appendices where full technical details are provided. Some questions are partially addressed in this revision; full formalization is scoped to future work.

\textbf{Q1. Formalization of precondition language and $\delta$/$\gamma$ derivation functions.} The precondition language is formalized in Section~\ref{subsec:formal-semantics} as a decidable fragment with $\mathcal{O}(1)$ evaluation: each precondition consists of a field path, a comparator from the closed \textsf{Comparator} enum, and a literal value, with explicit operator dispatch and no dynamic evaluation. The $\delta(C)$ and $\gamma(C)$ derivation functions are total computable functions implemented in $\mathcal{O}(n)$ time over the length of the chain. Neither the precondition language nor the derivation functions are Turing-complete; they are intentionally constrained to guarantee termination and predictability. (See~\S\ref{subsec:formal-semantics}.)

\textbf{Q2. Exact fork and merge rules; CRDT design for Phase~3.} Phase~1 fork semantics append a \texttt{FORK} event to the parent chain and initialize a child chain with a fresh \texttt{REGISTER} event bearing the parent \texttt{concept\_id} in its payload. Phase~1 merge is resolved by last-write-wins (LWW) on timestamp with \texttt{event\_id} as a deterministic tie-breaker; this is a social convention, not a CRDT. Theorem~\ref{thm:crdt-convergence} therefore applies only to the optional LWW-Set merge strategy described for Phase~3. A concrete CRDT design for EventChains in Phase~3 is outlined in the architecture: a Blocklace-style cryptographic hash DAG where ADL Lite's $\delta(C)$ and $\gamma(C)$ functions operate at the application layer above the convergent transport layer. (See~\S\ref{subsec:formal-semantics} and \S\ref{subsec:future-work}~FW4.)

\textbf{Q3. Machine-readable PROV-O/SHACL mapping with loss analysis.} Appendices~\ref{app:prov-o} and \ref{app:shacl} provide the precise, machine-readable mapping to PROV-O and SHACL. The mapping preserves events as PROV-O \texttt{Activity} instances, SHA-256 hashes as \texttt{adl:hash} datatype properties, event sequencing via \texttt{prov:wasInformedBy}, and actor identities as \texttt{prov:Agent} instances. What is not preserved in the translation are the executable $\delta$/$\gamma$ derivation functions, the precondition rule semantics, and the confidence aggregation algebra---these exist only in the ADL Lite application layer and have no direct PROV-O counterpart. Table~\ref{tab:loss-analysis} summarizes the preservation and loss profile.

\textbf{Q4. Actor identity management; Linked Data Proofs; equivocation prevention.} Phase~1 relies on self-declared string identifiers with collaborative community audit for collision and impersonation detection; replay attacks are mitigated by hash-chain integrity checks. Phase~3 will integrate Ed25519 signatures, decentralized identifiers (DIDs), and key transparency logs. Linked Data Proofs will bind signatures to Git commit objects, preserving the existing workflow while adding non-repudiation. Equivocation across forks is cryptographically attributable---the same key signing contradictory events produces detectable evidence of conflict---but it is not prevented by design; contradictory events are retained as explicit evidence of disagreement. (See~\S\ref{subsec:trust-model}.)

\textbf{Q5. Clean two-level account (occurrents vs records).} The ambiguity between EventChain as process and EventChain as information content entity is resolved in Section~\ref{sec:ontological-analysis} by a clean two-level account. The EventChain-process is an occurrent (a sequence of event occurrences) that produces the EventChain-record, which is an information content entity (ICE). The EventChain-record bears the Capability, which is a generically dependent continuant (GDC). There is no cross-level identity: the process and the record are distinct entities related by \textit{generates}. This removes the ambiguity without collapsing the distinction. (See~\S\ref{subsec:two-level-account}.)

\textbf{Q6. OWL ontology interoperability.} ADL Lite capabilities can be exported as OWL classes via the PROV-O mapping (Appendix~\ref{app:prov-o}). Status-derived properties (e.g., \texttt{adl:status}, \texttt{adl:confidence}) map to OWL annotation properties, and L3 relation blocks map to OWL object properties. The bidirectional bridge operates as follows: (1) ADL Lite $\rightarrow$ OWL: export an EventChain as PROV-O activities plus IAO information content entities; (2) OWL $\rightarrow$ ADL Lite: import an OWL class definition as a \texttt{REGISTER} event with a payload containing the OWL axioms. Full automation of this bridge is scoped to future work (FW1). (See~\S\ref{subsec:future-work}~FW1.)

\textbf{Q7. Extension of empirical study.} The empirical study reported in Section~\ref{sec:empirical-validation} has been extended to include multi-agent concurrency scenarios (E4d, E16), synthetic adversarial tampering (E9), scale-up projections to $10^5$--$10^6$ events (E6b, E13), and boundary-condition stress tests (E13--E16). Precondition and derivation coverage are reported as pass/fail rates per experiment, and failure modes (e.g., precondition timeout, hash mismatch, status derivation error, collusion vulnerability, contention rejection) are logged and categorized. The E6 AML pipeline is framed honestly: $96.8\%$ of events are \texttt{REGISTER} events derived from real transaction data, and only $3.2\%$ are synthetic governance events (\texttt{VALIDATE}, \texttt{RELATE}, \texttt{SEAL}) injected by the evaluation harness. The boundary experiments (E13--E16) quantify specific limits: linear verification scaling to $50$k events (E13), single-actor collusion dominance of confidence (E14), partial precondition coverage with Pydantic dependency (E15), and high rejection rates under contention without fork-on-conflict (E16). While the extended study strengthens architectural correctness claims and transparently reports negative results, real-world deployment at full scale remains unvalidated. (See~\S\ref{sec:empirical-validation}.)

\textbf{Q8. Formal semantics for relation lifecycle and UFO relators.} The formal semantics for relation lifecycle---creation via \texttt{RELATE} events, revocation via explicit deprecation or successor events, role constraints enforced by precondition rules, and temporal scoping via event timestamps---are presented in Section~\ref{sec:ontological-analysis}. These instantiate UFO relators as event-mediated social commitments: a \texttt{RELATE} event creates a relator (a set of qua individuals) that mediates between capabilities, and the relator's lifecycle is governed by subsequent events in the chain. This goes beyond the initial UFO alignment by making relator existence explicitly event-dependent. (See~\S\ref{subsec:relation-relator}.)

\subsection{Capability Registry Contribution}\label{subsec:conceptual-modeling}

ADL Lite contributes to capability registry practice in three ways.

\textbf{Layered capability documentation.} The L1--L4 document model provides a structured approach to capability representation that spans identity, narrative, assertion, and action. This layering is not merely a syntactic convenience; it reflects cognitive distinctions that humans (and LLMs) naturally make when describing capabilities. L1 answers ``what is it?''; L2 answers ``what is it about?''; L3 answers ``how does it relate to other capabilities?''; L4 answers ``what can be done with it?'' This four-layer structure can serve as a capability documentation methodology for domains beyond multi-agent capability governance.

\textbf{Event-driven capability lifecycle modeling.} Traditional capability modeling approaches (e.g., Entity-Relationship diagrams, UML class diagrams) represent lifecycle states as attributes or state machine diagrams. ADL Lite represents lifecycle states as event histories. This shift has methodological implications: the modeler must think in terms of ``what events can happen?'' rather than ``what states can the entity be in?'' This event-driven perspective is particularly valuable for domains with high capability velocity, where new event types emerge frequently.

\textbf{Collaborative capability governance.} The precondition system and closed action registry provide a lightweight mechanism for governing capability evolution. Unlike heavyweight governance frameworks (e.g., OWL ontology change management with reasoner validation), ADL Lite governance is declarative (YAML preconditions) and human-readable (Markdown documents). This lowers the barrier to collaborative capability authoring while maintaining machine-checkable constraints.

\subsection{Limitations}\label{subsec:limitations}

The results must be interpreted in light of ten specific limitations, each addressed by a corresponding Future Work item in Section~\ref{subsec:future-work}.

\textbf{L1. Informal foundational alignment.} The ontological analysis in Section~\ref{sec:ontological-analysis} is conceptual and narrative; it is not formalized as OWL~2~DL axioms, nor has it been validated with automated alignment tools. The mappings to BFO, DOLCE, and UFO are therefore subject to interpretation and may not support interoperability with ontologies in the OBO Foundry ecosystem. The two-level account (Section~\ref{subsec:two-level-account}) provides explicit identity and dependence axioms, but these are not machine-checked. (See~\S\ref{subsec:future-work}~FW1.)

\textbf{L2. Append-only accumulation and scale limits.} The append-only design treats every event as a permanent fact, creating three problems: junk accumulation from low-quality events, capability redundancy when multiple agents register semantically equivalent capabilities, and preference drift as deprecated capability definitions remain permanently visible. Quality gates, embedding-based deduplication, and controlled forgetting are all deferred to later phases. Additionally, while $10^5$--$10^6$ event feasibility is projected from linear scaling arguments (E6b, E13) and the $\mathcal{O}(n)$ complexity of $\delta(C)$ and $\gamma(C)$, empirical validation at that scale has not been performed. Memory pressure from hash-chain verification, I/O throughput for append-only logs, and concurrent agent contention at scale are all partially measured (E13: $50$k events; E16: $20$ agents) but not at production deployment scale. (See~\S\ref{subsec:future-work}~FW2.)

\textbf{L3. Un-calibrated confidence aggregation.} The confidence value in a \texttt{VALIDATE} event is self-reported by the actor. Literature in cognitive science and LLM evaluation demonstrates overconfidence: agents frequently report $\mathit{confidence} = 0.99$ when actual accuracy is materially lower. ADL Lite currently has no calibration mechanism, and Theorem~\ref{thm:boundedness} relies on these uncorrected values. Experiment E14 demonstrates that a single colluding actor can drive $\gamma(C)$ to $0.99$ through repeated self-validation, and Theorem~\ref{thm:boundedness} assumes non-colluding actors---a condition that E14 shows is violated in practice. (See~\S\ref{subsec:future-work}~FW3.)

\textbf{L4. Absence of cryptographic authentication and comparative baseline parity.} Phase~1 assumes collaborative audit among trusted participants identified only by string identifiers. There are no Linked Data Proofs, no decentralized identifiers (DIDs), and no tamper-evident actor authentication: a malicious agent could replay another agent's events or forge validator identities without detection. Byzantine fault tolerance is not addressed. This authentication gap also prevents head-to-head benchmark comparison against nanopublications and PROV-O pipelines, which provide built-in cryptographic signatures (RSA for nanopubs, LD-Proofs for PROV-O). Without authentication parity, performance and security comparisons are confounded by infrastructure differences rather than ontological design differences. The comparative evaluation in Section~\ref{subsec:comparative-eval} (E12) is therefore explicitly labeled as \emph{methodological rather than experimental}: it reports operational characteristics and published estimates, not head-to-head benchmark timings on identical tasks. (See~\S\ref{subsec:future-work}~FW4.)

\textbf{L5. Synthetic calibration without expert ground truth.} The AML evaluation (E1--E3, E4) mixes real transaction data with synthetically injected suspicious patterns. The detection logic has not been calibrated against expert labels, and no human annotators rated derived capabilities against independent quality criteria. The E6 pipeline is honestly framed: $96.8\%$ of events are \texttt{REGISTER} events derived from real transaction data, and only $3.2\%$ are synthetic governance events (\texttt{VALIDATE}, \texttt{RELATE}, \texttt{SEAL}) injected by the evaluation harness. Architectural correctness is therefore established, but real-world effectiveness remains unverified. (See~\S\ref{subsec:future-work}~FW5.)

\textbf{L6. Unstructured agent communication.} L2 Markdown body imposes no structural constraints on agent reasoning. Fixed reasoning templates (e.g., [Observation], [Reasoning], [Conclusion]) would provide stronger epistemic guarantees than lexical restrictions alone, but no template mechanism is implemented in Phase~1. (See~\S\ref{subsec:future-work}~FW6.)

\textbf{L7. Brittle capability discovery.} \texttt{DataImporter.discover\_classes()} relies solely on \texttt{\_id} suffix matching for capability class discovery. This heuristic is brittle (it misses classes without \texttt{\_id} suffixes), coverage-limited (it cannot discover relationships or attributes), and domain-dependent. Statistical clustering or LLM-based suggestion would improve quality. (See~\S\ref{subsec:future-work}~FW7.)

\textbf{L8. No Semantic Web interoperability.} ADL Lite has no native RDF/OWL export, no SPARQL endpoint, and no integration with graph databases. The L3 relation blocks and L4 action preconditions are inaccessible to standard Semantic Web tooling (GraphDB, RDF4J, Apache Jena), limiting reuse and interoperability. (See~\S\ref{subsec:future-work}~FW8.)

\textbf{L9. No incentive-compatible validation mechanism.} The current $\gamma(C)$ function aggregates self-reported confidence values without an incentive-compatible mechanism for truthful reporting. Validators have no economic or reputational stake in the accuracy of their assessments, and there is no penalty for systematic overconfidence. As demonstrated in E14, a single colluding actor can drive $\gamma(C)$ to $0.99$ through repeated self-validation, completely undermining the aggregate. (See~\S\ref{subsec:future-work}~FW9.)

\textbf{L10. Informal theorem proofs.} The seven theorems in Section~\ref{subsec:formal-semantics} are proved informally (rigorous natural-language arguments, not machine-checked derivations). While the proofs are complete and referenced in the text, they have not been verified in a proof assistant such as Coq or TLA+, leaving open the possibility of subtle gaps. (See~\S\ref{subsec:future-work}~FW10.)

\subsection{Complementary Systems}\label{subsec:complementary-systems}

KYA and AgentSafe are also complementary systems. KYA provides the trust boundary that ADL Lite's capability registry depends on; AgentSafe provides the architecture-level safety that constrains what capabilities may be exposed. ADL Lite adds the artifact-layer governance that records what capabilities are, how they evolve, and whether they are trustworthy.

ADL Lite's Git-native design provides branch-based concurrent authoring; what CRDTs would add is automatic merge semantics for event chains, eliminating the last-write-wins limitation without sacrificing tamper evidence. Blocklace \cite{blocklace2024} proposes a cryptographic hash DAG as a universal CRDT capable of detecting Byzantine nodes---a direction scoped to Phase~3. Unlike ADL Lite's linear SHA-256 chain (total order, $O(n)$ verification), Blocklace uses a directed acyclic graph (partial order, $O(|V|+|E|)$ verification) that supports concurrent event append without global coordination. Integrating Blocklace's DAG topology would upgrade ADL Lite's transport layer from linear chains to a partially ordered event graph while preserving the existing $\delta(C)$ and $\gamma(C)$ derivation semantics at the application layer.

\subsection{The Agent Governance Landscape}\label{subsec:agent-governance-landscape}

ADL Lite does not operate in isolation; it occupies a specific layer in the broader agent governance landscape. KYA answers ``Is this agent trusted?'' (permissions layer). AgentSafe answers ``Is this architecture safe?'' (design-time + runtime + audit). Talukdar et al. answer ``How do we build ontologies with agents?'' (production). ``From Agent Traces to Trust'' answers ``How do we trace what agents did?'' (provenance). SafeAgent answers ``Is this agent dangerous?'' (safety protocol). ADL Lite answers ``How do we record, validate, and govern the capabilities that agents advertise?'' (lifecycle registry). These layers are complementary, not competing. A complete agent ecosystem would use KYA for permissions, AgentSafe for architecture safety, and ADL Lite for capability lifecycle governance. No single system addresses all three concerns; the research contribution of ADL Lite is to make the capability layer explicit, auditable, and event-first.
